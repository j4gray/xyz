Graphs from The Singularity is Near by Ray Kurzweil

The technological singularity is a hypothesis that technological advancement is exponential and will eventually result in an intelligence explosion that will change life as we know it. Ray Kurzweil has made some predictions about the singularity (see my review of his bookThe Singularity is Near) and underlying these predictions is the understanding that as technology advances, it grows exponentially via bootstrapping (e.g. Computer-aided design technology is used to develop the latest circuit boards which enable more powerful design software). In order to fully embrace what that means you have to abandon the intuitive linear view we have when it comes to history; the progress of the last 10 years is not indicative of the future progress of the next 10 years, but rather the next 1 year. As far back as the Big Bang we can see progress is exponential and if you accept Kurzweil's assertion that technological advancment will eventually become a form of biological evolution, than tracking astronomical, biological, and technological milestones we can clearly see an exponential trajectory leading us to the singulairty. The graphs below show time before present on the x axis and time to the next event on the y axis and we can clearly see that there is exponentially less time between advances as we get closer to present day.


Note: all the graphs are plotted logarithmically. As a refresher look at the two exponential trajectories (y = 2x) plotted linearly vs logarithmically.

Paradigm Shifts pg. 19


This is a compliation of various lists composed by scholars that track human progress. Hover over the data points for a description of the advance. While there is some variety among the events, the trend is clear.






Canonical Milestones pg. 20


A condensed version of the paradigm shifts graphed above. Each data point represents a significant milestone in human evolution, starting with cosmic and biological events and transitioning to technological advances.






Moore's Law pg. 67


The most well-known example of exponential growth in technology was defined in 1965 by Gordon Moore, as the observation that the number of transistors in a dense integrated circuit doubles approximately every two years. This graph shows the processing power available to consumers for $1,000 in terms of calculations per second.






DNA sequencing cost (per genome) pg. 73


Our ability to extract relevant genomic data will play a significant role in the advent and uptake of personalized medicine, which will tailor therapy to patients according to biomarkers that suggest a powerful response. The cost of having your genome sequenced has fallen exponentially, paving the way for sophisticated data analysis to isolate biomarkers.




Kurzweil, Ray. The Singularity Is Near: When Humans Transcend Biology. New York: Penguin Books, 2006.
