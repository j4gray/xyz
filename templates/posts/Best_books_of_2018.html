Best Books of 2018

Jan 18 2018

I haven't written a blog post in a while so this is a dump of the best books I read in 2018 that I wanted to draw attention to (note: some of these books weren't published in 2018 that's just when I read them)



The Gene by Siddhartha Mukherjee

Siddhartha Mukherjee has followed up his best seller The Emperor of All Maladies: A Biography of Cancer with The Gene: An Intimate History and once again his abiliy to accesbily explain science is on full display in this comprehensive history of the human understanding of heredity. Along the way, Mukherjee made his central thesis as clear as day: from the beggining, human interest in ehredity and our understanding of it's mechanisms has been coupled with a desire to alter or otherwise control it - sometimes with dire consequences. As the field of genetics has evolved, it has been equally concerned with decording the information as with editing it and now as we enter into new frontiers of gene-editing technology (e.g. CRISPR) I think Mukherjee's primer is a must-read to keep current understanding and past efforts at eugenics in proper context.


Origin by Dan Brown

I was excited when I heard Dan Brown had released another installment in the Robert Landon series, having thouroughly enjoyed the first four and devoured them while on vacation, and I was pleasantly suprised to find upon reading Origin a novel I could recommend to people even if they weren't fans of the series. Moreover I found a solid portrayal of Artificial Intelligence in fiction that was entertaining, reasonably accurate, and extremely thought-provoking - not merely a beach read (although it doesn't disappoint as a quintessential thriller). The setting in Spain, where Landon must solve the murder of his friend and former student (the billionaire futurist Edomd Kirsch), provides a fabulous tension between past and future with the characers of the Spanish royal family, the Catholic Church, the references to Gaudi and Brown's masterful writing of 'Watson' - Kirsch's superintelligent AI asisstant. I was worried that Brown would succumb to the same pitfall as many writers who have attempted to write AI charcters - especially superintelligent AI characters - whereby the plot becomes so complicated that the only possible climax is a deus ex machina, but I am delighted to say that the novel has no such flaws.


How to Change Your Mind by Michael Pollan

I found out about this book, a review of the clincial application of psychedelics, when Sam Harris had Michael Pollan as a guest on his podcast to discuss it; having read and greatly enjoyed Waking Up where Harris described some of his own experiences on psychedelics as well as two of Pollans's books (The Botany of Desire and The Omnivore's Dilema) I was suitably intrigued to buy the book before they started talking and after they were finished talking I was positively sold. The story of psychedelics in the United States is almost entirely dominated with discussion of Timothy Leary and Ken Kasey's acid tests but I had no idea that at the same time as Leary was encouraging people to to tune in and drop out there was serious clinical research going on at places like John's Hopkins that were finding extremely promising results using compounds like LSD ans psylocybin to treat alocoholism. This research was stopped as part of the cultural backlash against psychelics in the last 1960s and early 1970s. It was only very recently that researchers have revisted the research and tried to replicate those early studies with astounding results - for example in cases of smoking cessation. Additionally, new understanding of the biochemistry of psychelics has renewed scientific interest and Pollan's chapter on the neroscience of chemicals like LSD and psylocybin is by far my favorite part in the book - if you don't know about the default mode network, prepare to have your mind blown


Bad Blood by John Carreyrou

This was the book I most enjoyed reading in 2018. Even though I was very familiar with the story of Elizabeth Holmes and her fradulent blood testing technology Theranos, having followed it from it's inception as a Silicon Valley unicorn to the largest corporate fraud in American business since Enron, I couldn't put this book down and I read it in almost a single sitting over the weekend that it was released. If you are not familiar with Holmes, you will find yourself in a state of disbelief at just how expanisve the fraud was and how long she was able to maintain it. Carreyrou was the journalist who first broke news of fradulent activity at Theranos in a series of articles starting in October 2015 for the Wall Street Journal, and he delights in filling in the details of the early days of the company and insterting himself as a character in the story when he was contacted by a whistle-blower. After reading the book I went through adn watched some of Holmes' more famous public appearences including her TED talk when Theranos was still the darling of Silicon Valley as well as her interview on Mad Money immediately following Carreyrou's first piece to remind myself that this is not some work of thriller ficiton.
