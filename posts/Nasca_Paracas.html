Nazca & Paracas

Jul 30 2016

I took a lovely nightbus from Arequipa to Nazca through Cruz del Sur, which offered "VIP serice" for $7 extra and included a fully reclining seat and personal entertainment system. I arrived in Nazca early in the morning and went to the office of the airline I had contact to drop my luggage and confirm my flight. The Nazca Lines were made between 500BC and 700AD by the Nazca people who carved the gigantic geometric and biomorphic designs into the desert for an unknown purpose.


The design and placement of the lines has been a source of continuous interest with theories ranging from trailmarkers to sacred pathways, astronomical observation, and extraterrestrial contact. This last theory most likely born from the anthropomorphic figure known as the 'astronaut' who bears a resemblence to some popular conceptions of aliens.


I headed on to Paracas to spend the night and encountered yet another stunning climate: a desert beachtown sitting with access to the Islas Ballestas and Paracas National Park where you could observe the diverse wildlife including penguins, boobies, comorants, and sea lions, as well as the red-and-white flamigos that are said to have inspired the Peruvian flag.


Also visible from the boat tour to the Islas Ballestas is the enoumous cactus-shape Candelbra geoglyph, which, like the Nazca Lines, has been preserved by the climate and its positioning that shields it from the wind. Once again, no one knows why it was created with theories including a representation of the power of northern Chavín culture and a Freemason symbol placed their by General José de San Martín (the leader of the liberation movement in Peru).
