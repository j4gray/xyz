Rest in peace Hans Rosling

Feb 8 2017


Yesterday, Hans Rosling, a Swedish global health professor famed for his TED talks and presentation of statistics as dynamic visualizations, which in turn tell powerful stories, died at the age of 68. He was a great hero of mine for demonstrating exactly how powerful statistics can be when communicated effectively through visualizations. As a neuroscience student, I learned that the visual cortex is the largest system in the human brain, which means we are hard wired to think with our eyes and our ability to intake information visually dwarfs any other comminication capacity we have. Statistics all too often are dull tools that communicate percentages and rates of change as abstract numbers instead of utilizing the rich processing power we have in our visual cortex. Instead of a number representing percent change, show me a trend line and I will internalize what I see because it is the natural course of action for my brain.


I first encountered Rosling's presentations in high school when I was procrastinating doing my homework by watching TED talks (which I had convinced myself could be thought of as equally academic as homework) and I found one that was called "The best stats you've ever seen." Immediately, I, along with millions of others (the video now has over 11 million views on the TED website) was entralled with the way this man painted a picture with data that was not only clear and entertaining but challenged my preconieved notions about the world to the core. He used data to shape my worldview and he was successful because of how well he communicated it. I ended up minoring in Global Health in college and have since watched all of his TED talks and he continues to be an inspiration to me so I compiled the presentations that were formative in shaping and challenging my conception of the world. Hans Rosling, thank you for everything, you will be sorely missed.

The best stats you've ever seen


(2006)


Using life expectancy and fertility rate (average number of children per woman) of countries around the world from 1960 to 2003, Rosling challenges the idea that there are there is a difference between the 'Western world' and the 'Third world.'





Insights on HIV


(2009)


Subsaharan Africa is home to only 4% of the world population, however accounts for 50% of HIV infected persons. Rosling shows that the story of HIV in these countries is not monolithic and there are multiple risk factors at play.





The good news of the decade? We're winning the war against child mortality


(2010)


The UN millenium goals consist of 8 specific targets to address extreme poverty. Rosling argues that the quanitziation of these goals is overly holistic and does not accurately reflect the progress of the last decade.





The magic washing machine


(2010)


Rosling argues that the washing machine was the greatest invention of the industrial revolution using energy usage in the world.





Global population growth, box by box


(2010)


In the next 50 years, the global population will grow to 9 billion and that growth will continue unless we raise the living standards of the poorest group because population growth is driven by large families which are in turn driven by infant mortality and access to maternal healthcare (including birth control).





Religion and babies


(2012)


Does religion play a role in family size? Rosling argues that the widespread cultural phenomenon of reduction in family size is not tied to religion but rather average income. As the global population ages we will see a filling in of the age distribution and the peak human population will be 10 billion people.
